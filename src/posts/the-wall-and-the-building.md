In April I drafted an essay with a confident claim: model releases had stopped mattering. Opus 4.7 and GPT-5.5 were both out, I wrote, and I could barely articulate what either did better in my actual work. The benchmarks showed a plateau, the physics explained it, and the smart money was on the scaffolding around the models rather than the models themselves. I didn't publish it. I'm glad about that now, because it was wrong in a way worth dissecting in public.

The plateau I cited was real for about five months. Through the winter, the top models on SWE-bench Verified sat within a point or two of each other at around 80%, and it was easy to read that as a ceiling. Then Opus 4.7 posted 87.6% on April 16 and GPT-5.5 posted 88.7% on April 23, one week and two weeks before my essay's own date. I quoted both models in the text while calling them marginal. On June 9, Anthropic released [Fable 5](https://www.latent.space/p/ainews-anthropic-claude-fable-5-mythos) at a reported 95.0% on Verified and 80.3% on SWE-Bench Pro, the contamination-resistant successor benchmark where I had claimed the frontier was stuck near 23%. That 23% figure was already seven months stale when I used it. The plateau didn't survive six weeks past my draft. It didn't even survive until my draft.

The part that stings is the reason it fooled me. The flat winter numbers came mostly from a standardized, deliberately cheap evaluation harness. The plateau was measuring the scaffold as much as the model, and my own essay argued that scaffolding dominates outcomes. I had the mechanism in my hands and still read the chart as a capability ceiling. What followed my draft was three releases in five weeks, each wrong-footing the forecast in a different direction, and the three of them together redraw the map I was trying to draw. This is the corrected version.

## The Wall

The part of the April essay I'd still defend is the physics.

Start with the framing I stole from John Salvatier's 2017 essay "Reality Has a Surprising Amount of Detail." The physical world is fractally complex, and experts handle that complexity by baking it into intuition. A master carpenter doesn't reason through wood warp explicitly. For the carpenter, it's transparent, felt, built in. An LLM has no such luxury. To hold a constraint across a long task, it has to say the constraint out loud, because generating tokens is its cognitive processing. If it doesn't write it down, it isn't thinking about it.

This is literal, and in April, Anthropic proved it the hard way. Their [post-mortem of a month-long Claude Code quality collapse](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/april-23-postmortem) traced the damage to three compounding harness changes: a reasoning-effort downgrade, a caching bug that dropped reasoning history between turns, and a system-prompt line capping text between tool calls at 25 words. That verbosity cap alone cost a measurable 3% on one coding evaluation. The model wasn't being chatty when it narrated. It was thinking. Cut the narration and the details evaporate.

The deeper problem is what that narration costs physically. Inference has two phases. Prefill reads your prompt with the GPU near full utilization. Then decoding generates tokens one at a time, and for each new token the architecture has to scan the entire conversation history stored as the Key-Value cache. Longer context means a bigger cache means more data physically moving from VRAM to the processor on each step. Memory bandwidth grows far more slowly than compute, so during decoding, GPU utilization often drops below 10%. The cores sit idle, starved, because the memory can't push data fast enough. Making the model larger makes it worse: more parameters, more cache, more traffic per token.

There's a second wall behind the first. When a model acts as an agent, pausing to run scripts and call tools, the GPU waits on the CPU. [One analysis of agentic execution](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.00739) found tool-side CPU processing eating 50 to 90% of end-to-end latency, with the 88% figure I cited in April being the upper bound of one tool-heavy setup rather than a typical case. The GPU sits idle while a sequential Python script shuffles state around.

Two walls, both of them physical, and brute force hits both of them head-on. That much I got right. What I got wrong was what the industry would do about it.

## The Routing

My April forecast was that the architectural workarounds, things like compressed attention, sliding windows, and sparse mixture-of-experts, would make intelligence cheaper and faster but not qualitatively greater. The routing happened exactly as predicted. The outcome did not.

Look at [Kimi K3](https://www.kimi.com/blog/kimi-k3), released this week and basically a dramatization of what the wall predicted. Moonshot built it around Kimi Delta Attention, a hybrid linear attention that decodes up to 6.3x faster at million-token context because it mostly stops rescanning the whole conversation. It activates 16 of 896 experts per token, roughly 50 billion active parameters by outside estimates, out of 2.8 trillion total. It was trained quantization-aware, so 4-bit weights aren't a compression of the model, they're the reference precision it was built at. Moonshot claims about 2.5x better scaling efficiency from the architecture. And then, this is the part my forecast missed, they spent the savings on scale: nearly triple the parameters of their previous model, landing within a few points of the frontier on the [Artificial Analysis index](https://artificialanalysis.ai/models/comparisons/kimi-k3-vs-gpt-5-6-sol), 57 to Fable's 60, at $3 per million input tokens.

Fable 5 made the same point from the other direction. It's reportedly twice the size of Opus, a genuinely bigger model, and its gains are real even if the headline numbers are vendor-reported and contested. Karpathy called it a step change. Stripe has a marketing anecdote about migrating a 50-million-line Ruby codebase in a day, which I cite strictly as a marketing anecdote, but the shape of the result, that the gains concentrate on long, hard, multi-day tasks, is corroborated by how the model behaves everywhere else. OpenAI's [GPT-5.6 Sol](https://openai.com/index/gpt-5-6/), meanwhile, pitches efficiency as the headline: the same work in less than half the output tokens. No lab is shipping denser-is-better anymore. They're shipping physics-aware machines, and the machines keep getting smarter.

So the correction to April: efficiency in this industry is capability with a lag. The savings from routing around the wall don't get banked as cheapness. They get reinvested into the next training run, the next sparsity ratio, the next context length. The wall didn't end scaling. It changed what scaling is made of.

## The Harness Got Eaten

The strongest claim in the April draft was that the real gains had moved to the scaffolding: the harnesses, tools, and workflows wrapped around the model. The evidence was good then and it's better now. [LangChain moved their coding agent from 52.8% to 66.5% on Terminal Bench 2.0](https://www.langchain.com/blog/improving-deep-agents-with-harness-engineering) with the model pinned in place, changing only the harness. [Vercel cut their agent's toolset from 18 tools to 2](https://vercel.com/blog/we-removed-80-percent-of-our-agents-tools) and watched success go from 80% to 100%, on a five-query eval, so grain of salt, but the direction matches the rest of the evidence.

My mistake was the conclusion I drew: that you should watch the harnesses, not the model releases. Watch what the labs actually shipped this summer. Sol's headline feature is Ultra mode, which coordinates four communicating subagents in parallel as a model setting, and its vendor-reported Terminal-Bench score jumps from 88.8% to 91.9% when you turn it on. Its best-known coding result, an 80 on the Coding Agent Index, was achieved with max reasoning inside OpenAI's own scaffold. Moonshot sells a K3 variant, Swarm Max, built to orchestrate hundreds of subagents. The labs looked at the same evidence I did and reached the obvious commercial conclusion: if the gains live in the harness, ship the harness as part of the model.

So the dichotomy I built the essay on, model versus harness, dissolved in about eight weeks. What you buy now is the merged system, benchmarked inside its own scaffolding, which means each benchmark number ships with an asterisk about whose scaffold produced it. The releases are the harnesses. And access to the merged system is itself becoming gated: Sol previewed to a short list of government-cleared partners, and Fable 5 was [suspended for 19 days in June](https://www.cio.com/article/4191550/us-reverses-export-restrictions-on-anthropics-fable-5-mythos-5-ai-models.html) after an export-control directive, then restored. Distribution used to mean an API key, and increasingly it means a flag.

## The Price Ladder

Here's what intelligence costs as of this week. OpenAI's Luna tier: $1 per million input tokens, $6 per million output. Kimi K3, open weights, near-frontier: $3 and $15, with cache reads at $0.30. GPT-5.6 Sol: $5 and $30. Claude Fable 5, the top: $10 and $50. On the Artificial Analysis composite, K3, Sol, and Fable sit at 57, 59, and 60.

The structure of that ladder is the story. The good-enough tier keeps rising while collapsing in price: an open-weight model within 3 points of the best proprietary system costs 30% as much per token. The frontier still holds a premium, 3.3x the price for about 3 points of measured intelligence, and whether that premium is worth paying depends entirely on failure cost. On long-horizon work where Fable succeeds in one pass and the cheaper model needs two, the premium partly pays for itself. For high-volume work where retries are cheap, it doesn't. Routing tasks across tiers is becoming an ordinary engineering decision, like choosing instance sizes.

And if you don't like the prices, you can buy the building outright. I ran the numbers on self-hosting K3 when the weights drop later this month. The 4-bit weights are about 1.5TB, so the smallest honest rig is 8 B300s at [$59 an hour on RunPod](https://www.runpod.io/pricing), or 16 H200s across two nodes at $70. BF16 wants 6.5TB of VRAM, starts around $211 an hour, and buys you perhaps a point of accuracy, because the model was trained at 4-bit in the first place. Throughput is the real killer: rigs serving this class of model deliver 25 to 50 tokens a second per stream, and [RunPod's own K2 guide](https://www.runpod.io/blog/guide-to-moonshotais-kimi-k2-on-runpod) measured 40 to 52. Moonshot's API is observed around 27. The 140 tokens per second I naively budgeted for does not exist at any price. To beat Moonshot's $15 per million output tokens on cost, you'd need to sustain something like 1,100 output tokens per second, 24 hours a day, all month. Owning the commodity costs more than renting it until you're basically a utility. Models lose money. The building wins. Which turns out to be the labs' problem too, at a much larger scale.

## The Building

I've been circling the business version of this for weeks, and the summer's releases have only sharpened it. "Are the AI labs profitable" is a malformed question, because there are three layers of profitable and the answer flips at each one.

The first is gross margin on inference: revenue per token minus the cost of generating it. That's genuinely positive, often in the 50 to 80% range. Selling tokens makes money. The second is operating margin: inference profit minus research, talent, and stock comp. That's where it goes negative for most labs. The third is net of the next training run, and that's where the structure lives. Dario Amodei's own math, in a [long interview](https://dwarkesh.com/p/dario-amodei-2): a model that cost a billion to train brings in four billion and costs a billion to serve. That model makes money. But the company is spending ten billion training the next one, so the company loses money. Both things are true at once, by construction.

The reason it can't resolve is a pair of scissors. [The compute required to train a frontier model grows 4 to 5x a year](https://epoch.ai/blog/training-compute-of-frontier-ai-models-grows-by-4-5x-per-year). [The price of inference at a fixed capability level falls about 10x a year](https://epoch.ai/data-insights/llm-inference-price-trends). The thing they sell gets cheaper every year, while the thing they must buy gets bigger every year. This is exactly the dynamic the inference wall describes, seen from the balance-sheet side: the physics that made me wrong about capability is the same physics making intelligence a commodity.

Joel Spolsky named the classic response: [commoditize your complement](https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2002/06/12/strategy-letter-v/). You don't capture value in the layer going cheap. You capture it in the adjacent layer that stays scarce. So the labs are fleeing into different scarce complements. Google went down, into chips: TPUs put their unit cost of compute something like 40% below anyone paying Nvidia's margin. Meta went sideways: Llama is a complement to the ads business and funded by it, and profitability is a category error there. Cohere went up, into enterprise applications at 70% gross margins, and its co-founder literally wrote the transformer paper. And then there's the building itself. xAI's Colossus site in Memphis ran Grok at around 11% utilization, so in February SpaceX absorbed xAI, and three months later Anthropic leased the entire Colossus 1, over 200,000 GPUs, at $1.25 billion a month through 2029. The model couldn't fill the building, so the building went to work for the competition. Follow the money one more step and you land on the hyperscalers: the five biggest infrastructure players will spend something like $670 billion on data centers this year, and the labs' record funding rounds flow mostly to the same handful of companies that wrote the checks.

I'd love to end that argument with "so the escape is the application layer," but I live in the application layer, and it doesn't hold either. My own development workflow, a spec, plan, build, verify, retro loop that turns an unreliable model into a reliable system, is already shipped by Cursor and Claude Code and, as of this month, baked into the models themselves as Ultra and Swarm modes. The thing that makes a model reliable also makes it replaceable, and replaceable things commoditize. From the model layer on up through the workflow layer, each tier above the silicon is dissolving. What's left holding margin is what you can't copy: compute, power, chips, locked-in distribution, and, new this summer, sovereign approval.

## Admission

So the two drafts turn out to be one essay. Capability kept climbing and prices kept collapsing for the same reason, which is why the April plateau was fake but the commoditization is real. Physics became economics, and economics became structure: a commodity tier converging on free, a frontier tier holding its premium, and a physical layer collecting the money from both.

For the labs, the conclusion hasn't moved. The model was the cost of admission, and the admission price is a training run that token revenue doesn't cover. For the rest of us, admission got cheaper and stranger at the same time. Near-frontier intelligence costs $3 a million tokens and fits in 1.5TB of 4-bit weights, while the best available costs 3.3x that and comes with government review. The practical skill of the next few years isn't picking a model. It's routing: knowing which tier each task deserves, and paying the frontier premium only where failure costs more than tokens.

As for me, I'm holding the position I staked out before any of this summer's releases: what stays scarce isn't intelligence, it's specifying what done means and standing behind the result. The wall set the physics, the physics set the prices, and the prices are quietly deciding who gets to stay in the room.
